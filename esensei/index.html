E-sensei

Product page for an algorithm that helps e-sports teams improve. Made while working at QED Software for one of the company's AI solutions.

See the website here.

Summary

The website was intended to present the project to possible partners that could benefit from it. That's why the main focus of the project was to display the most important aspects of the product in a visually appealing way.

I was given the content of the website (text and structure) and needed to create a design with the communications manager at the company. The website was implemented by a freelance front-end developer. The vector graphics were made by the company's graphic designer after I briefed him on what will be needed for the website.

Design problem

The most important issue was grabbing attention. The texts I was given presented the product well, but bullet points and descriptions are not easy when scanning a website — that's why we needed to come up with visual ways of presenting the ideas.

One of the problems I noticed after I started working at QED was the inconsistent presentation of the company's portfolio. I wanted to change that. We discussed the problem with the communications manager and later I came up with some general visual guidelines that the website would take after other products.

Visual concepts

Esensei is a product aimed at gamers, so one of the main themes should be gaming. At the same time it's an AI product and the company wanted to emphasise the tech behind it, therefore, we wanted to give the site a tech theme. The last element was the Sensei — it was part of the teaching metaphor and was crucial to the project brand, so the motif had to be used.

I came up with a couple basic layouts for the website. Now when I see them, I have no idea how I thought somebody would pick the first ones, so I included only two (the winner included). I chose a subtle font (Barlow) to be easy on the eye, but not to overshadow the main visual components. For the colors I decided to go for something vibrant, gamers often love RGB lights, so I thought I would play with this idea.

I planned an illustration for the second design in the style of another illustration by Davide Mazzuchin on Dribbble.

Design decisions and brief

Along with the communications manager we decided to go with the last design. We went through all the diagrams and ilustrations needed – I suggested how they could look and later, after another discussion, we gave the graphic designer a brief with some direction. Here is an example of the designs used in the brief.

Visual consistency

QED Software employs many teams working on different project, but the website these projects produce don't look like they are from the same company. That's why I took some design solutions from other websites the company created. One of such decision was the slide structure taken from one project that's also a product for gaming.

Final design

I designed the website with the communications manager, but had to wait for the final illustrations for a long time. That's where the illustrations used in the brief came in handy; I could already design everything on a grid and later replace the illustrations with the final ones.

The illustrations (although time-consuming) really made the website complete. They diluted the text-heavy presentation and gave a game-related but also hi-tech feeling.
